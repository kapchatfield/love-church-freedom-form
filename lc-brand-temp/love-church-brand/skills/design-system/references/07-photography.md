# 07 — Photography

Photography in the Love Church system has two modes — **documentary candid** and **B&W halftone hero** — plus a tertiary **studio portrait** mode for leadership and feature subjects. There's no glossy, no stock, no airbrushed influencer aesthetic. The brand's photography has been built around the church's actual rooms, actual people, actual moments.

## The two primary modes

### Mode 1 — Documentary candid (the default)

The default photo treatment. Color, natural light, captured-in-the-moment. Emotional rather than staged.

**What it looks like:**
- Worship moments — hands raised, eyes closed, faces lit by stage light
- Lobby and foyer encounters — handshakes, conversations, kids in the family area
- Outreach — Love Out Loud volunteers in the field, in-context
- Group settings — circles, prayer huddles, dinner tables

**Color treatment:**
- Slight warm cast (not a heavy Instagram filter — a subtle warming of skin tones and stage tungsten).
- Natural saturation. Don't crush blacks; don't blow highlights. Editorial, not contrasty.
- Skin tones true. The Love Out Loud spread (page 14 of the Annual) shows the canonical color science: warm but not yellow, saturated but not punchy.

**When to use:**
- Default editorial photography across the system
- IG feed worship shots (the @lovechurchomaha grid is dominated by these)
- Web hero rotation
- MailChimp body imagery

### Mode 2 — B&W halftone hero

The cover treatment. Used when a piece needs gravity and stillness instead of warmth. The Annual Report cover is the definitive example — raised hands, B&W, halftone grain, dark.

**What it looks like:**
- Photo desaturated fully to grayscale
- Halftone or grain overlay — a tactile, print-y texture (~3–5% noise on top of dot-screen)
- Black point lifted slightly (~10–15% gray as the deepest black) so the print feels paperish, not digital
- Text in `lc-cream` set on top — high contrast, heroic

**When to use:**
- Annual Report covers and chapter dividers
- Sermon series art when the series tone is reflective or weighty
- IG quote cards where the photo is supporting the line, not competing with it
- Email hero when a launch needs gravity

**When NOT to use:**
- Joyful event posts (Sundays at Love recaps, REV Rally) — the warmth of color is the point
- Kids ministry imagery (color always — the audience is parents, and parents see their kids in color)
- Anything where the audience needs to see a person's clothing or product clearly (like merch, conference signage)

### Mode 3 — Studio portrait

Used for leadership reveals, single-subject features, and the Annual's "More To Life Story" portraits.

**What it looks like:**
- Subject on white seamless or muted gray seamless
- Even, soft lighting (a single large source + fill)
- Shot wide enough to include hands and posture (Pastor Mike's commission portrait shows the full pose, not a tight head-and-shoulders)
- Color natural, slightly warmer than neutral

**When to use:**
- Leadership transitions and feature pieces
- More To Life Story headshots (subject + their context — no seamless required for these; can be on-location)
- Press-style portraits when a single person needs to be the subject

## Crop logic

| Aspect | Use |
|---|---|
| 4:5 portrait | IG feed default. Centerline subjects. |
| 9:16 vertical | Stories, reels, vertical video. Fill the frame. |
| 16:9 landscape | YT thumbnails, web heroes. Subject in left 60%. |
| 1:1 square | Carousel slides, app cards. Subjects centered. |
| Full bleed | Annual covers, web heroes — let the photo be the canvas. |

**Subject placement:**
- **Eyes on the upper third.** Whether the crop is portrait or landscape, the subject's eyes generally land on the upper-third horizontal. This is editorial standard.
- **Negative space is a feature.** Don't fill every corner. The Annual's worship cover has more dark/empty space than subject — that's why the headline lands.
- **Hands matter.** Worship photography in this system shows hands deliberately — raised, clasped, lifted. Don't crop them out.

## Overlays and treatments

When type sits on a photo:

- **Cream overlay (5–15% opacity black wash)** — used on dark photos when the type still needs more contrast. Applied as a gradient from the type's edge fading into transparency.
- **Pink corner pill** — drops in the year/CTA pill component over a photo when needed (Annual cover does this).
- **Vignette** — never. The system uses gradient overlays where needed; vignettes feel cheap.
- **Drop shadows on type** — never. If the photo doesn't have enough contrast for the type to read, treat the photo (gradient overlay) or move the type.

## What's out of system

- Stock photography of generic "happy people." If the photo isn't of *Love Church people in Love Church spaces* or has been licensed for similar use, it's out.
- Heavy color filters (Lo-Fi, Mayfair, Valencia, etc.). The brand has its own subtle warmth; filters override it.
- Tilt-shift, fish-eye, extreme bokeh. Editorial restraint applies to lens choice too.
- Posed group shots with everyone facing camera. The brand doesn't do "say cheese." It does *moment*.

## Direction prompts (for when AI image generation is appropriate)

When generating image direction for a designer or for an AI image tool, structure the prompt:

```
[Mode]. [Subject + action]. [Lighting]. [Crop]. [Color treatment].
```

Example for an IG feed announcement:

> Documentary candid. Worship leader at microphone, eyes closed, mid-song. Stage light from behind, warm tungsten. 4:5 portrait crop, subject centered. Natural color, slight warm cast, no filter.

Example for an Annual cover:

> B&W halftone hero. Crowd shot from behind, hands raised toward stage. Stage haze backlit. Full-bleed 8.5×11 portrait. Halftone grain texture overlay, black point lifted ~15%. High-contrast cream type will sit centered.

## Pre-flight check

Before delivering a photo spec:

- Is this on one of the three modes (documentary, B&W halftone, studio)?
- Does the photo show Love Church people / spaces or is it stock? If stock, flag it.
- Is the crop right for the surface?
- If type sits on the photo, is there a treatment to ensure contrast?
- Did I avoid filters, heavy color grades, and posed groups?

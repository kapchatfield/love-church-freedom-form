# Sermon Structuring Checklist

Before generating any content, spend a few minutes mapping the sermon. The quality of every downstream deliverable depends on this map. If you skip it, you'll end up writing carousel slides off of keyword search, and they'll sound like it.

## The short map

Fill this out mentally (or in a scratch file) before you write anything for the user.

1. **Thesis** — one sentence the preacher could agree with: "The one thing this message is trying to do is __." If you can't write this sentence, read the transcript again.
2. **Primary text** — the scripture(s) the preacher is teaching *from* (as opposed to quoting in passing). Usually one passage the sermon returns to.
3. **Secondary texts** — other verses or passages referenced. Note them in order.
4. **Main illustration(s)** — the story, metaphor, or image that carries the weight. In most sermons there's one big one plus a few smaller ones. The big one becomes a short-form clip candidate; the smaller ones go in carousel slides.
5. **Structural points** — the preacher's own outline. This might be stated ("three principles") or implicit. Either way, reverse-engineer it. These become YouTube chapters and carousel slide ordering.
6. **Repeated phrases** — the one or two lines the preacher repeated for emphasis. These are *always* quote-graphic candidates. Don't miss them.
7. **Felt questions** — the "have you been there?" "does this sound familiar?" moments. These are hook material for short-form and carousel slide 1.
8. **The altar move** — what the preacher invited people *into* at the end. This shapes the invitational CTA across the kit — it should feel consistent with how the message actually closed.

## Timestamp anchoring

For every major moment on your map, note the timestamp from `transcript.txt`. You'll reuse these when writing:

- YouTube chapter markers (every major structural shift).
- Short-form clip windows (one or two per major illustration and per repeated phrase).
- Pull-quote attribution if the kit is embedded somewhere timestamped.

Don't round aggressively. `[12:30]` is fine; `[13:00]` when the real line starts at `[12:47]` will frustrate the editor.

## Quick smell tests

After mapping, ask yourself:

- If someone read only the quote graphics, would they get the thesis?
- If someone watched only the short-form clips, would they understand the stakes?
- If someone read only the blog recap, would they know what the preacher invited them into?

If any answer is "no," your map is missing something. Reread those sections of the transcript and fix it before you write the kit.

## When the transcript is thin

Some messages are more conversational than structured. If the sermon doesn't have a clear three-point outline, don't fake one. Structure the YouTube chapters around emotional beats (setup, tension, turn, call) instead. Structure the carousel around the preacher's own through-line. The audience will tell you you invented scaffolding that wasn't there.

## When the preacher misquotes scripture or is ambiguous

If the preacher references a verse you can tell is paraphrased, cite the actual reference in the blog and email (where readers may look it up) but quote the preacher's phrasing in short-form and carousels (where authenticity matters more than precision). If you can't verify a reference, omit it rather than guessing.

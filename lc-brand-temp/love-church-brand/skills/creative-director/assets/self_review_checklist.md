# Self-Review Checklist
**Run this on every piece BEFORE you submit it for review.**

If you can't honestly check all six categories, the piece isn't ready. Bringing in unfinished work doesn't get you faster feedback — it gets you the same feedback you could've given yourself.

---

## The Six Squint Tests

For each box, **answer plainly: yes or no.** No hedging.

### ☐ Composition
**Squint at the piece. Is there a grid you can see?** Are the margins intentional, or did the elements land where they landed? Negative space is a tool — are you using it, or did it just happen?
> If the piece would fall apart when you turn off all the photos and effects: composition isn't there yet.

### ☐ Color
**Could you remove one color and the piece would be stronger?**
If yes — remove it now. If you have more than three colors, you have too many. Check contrast on every text element (white text on a busy photo without a scrim does not pass).

### ☐ Typography
**Are you using one type family — at most two?** Is every typeface here doing a job no other typeface in the piece could do? An "effect" (chrome, gradient, drop shadow, outline) is **not** a type choice. If your headline only works with the effect on, the headline isn't done.

### ☐ Hierarchy
**Cover the headline. Can you still tell what this is about?**
If yes, hierarchy is broken — fix the secondary read. The eye should know which element to read first, second, third — and not have to choose between two equal-weight elements.

### ☐ Storytelling
**In one sentence, what is this piece about?** Write it down. If the sentence is generic ("a sermon series," "a volunteer ask"), the piece is generic too. Try again with a specific verb.

### ☐ Emotional Impact
**Name the target emotion in one word.** (Awe. Conviction. Invitation. Urgency. Comfort. Joy. Sobriety.) Now look at the piece. Does it earn that emotion *before* you read the words? If you don't feel anything, the piece doesn't feel anything either.

---

## The Three Killers — kill these every time

Most weak submissions have at least one of these. Catch them yourself before someone else does.

1. **Three-typeface syndrome.** A headline face, a subhead face from a different family, and a CTA in a third face. Pick one family. Use weights to do what you were trying to do with different fonts.
2. **The default photo.** Stock photo of hands on a Bible / smiling diverse circle / sun-flare worship moment. If your piece could illustrate any other church's content with no changes, it isn't yours yet.
3. **The opacity overlay band-aid.** A 30% blue or black overlay slapped over a busy photo to make white text legible. The overlay never solves it. Either change the photo, change the type placement, or use a real darkened gradient that's part of the design.

---

## The 30-Second Test

Before you submit:

- **Show it to someone for 3 seconds, then take it away.** Ask them what it was about. If they can't tell you, hierarchy or storytelling is broken — go back.
- **Put the piece next to something it'll live near** (the lobby slide deck, the IG feed grid, last week's sermon graphic). Does it belong, or does it sit awkwardly? Belonging is not blandness — a strong piece holds its place in a system.
- **Read the piece out loud.** Headline, subhead, CTA. Does it sound like a person made it? Or does it sound like a template?

---

## What "ready to submit" actually means

You've done all six squint tests honestly. You've killed the three killers. You've done the 30-second test. The piece is the best version you can make today **with the information you currently have.**

Ready does not mean perfect. It means: *you've used your own discipline before asking for someone else's.*

---

## A note on speed

This checklist takes 5 minutes to run. The reviews you save by running it will save you hours. Skipping it doesn't make you faster — it makes the team slower, because someone else now has to do the diagnostic work you skipped.

Run it every time.

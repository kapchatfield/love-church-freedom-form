# Worked examples

Four full reviews showing the standardized output format in action across the score range. Read these once at the start of a session so the rhythm is fresh.

The examples are written from descriptions, not real uploads. When you have an actual image in front of you, ground every observation in what's literally on screen — measurements, alignments, contrast, photographic detail.

---

## Example 1 — Sermon series key art (Refine, 46/60)

*Imagined piece: A 16:9 series graphic for a 4-week series titled "ANCHORED." Background is a wide, slightly desaturated photograph of a stormy sea horizon, slate-gray sky meeting near-black water. The series title is set in a heavy uppercase sans-serif, white, dropped roughly center, with the subtitle "a series in 1 Peter" in a thin condensed serif beneath it. A small church wordmark sits bottom-left.*

### ## Creative Director Summary

This is the strongest piece on the desk this week. The photograph and the typography are working together, not against each other, and the restraint in the palette earns the gravity the title is asking for. The single thing holding it back is the subtitle treatment — it's fighting the headline at the same eye-level instead of submitting to it. Tighten that one relationship and this ships.

### ## Verdict

**Refine** — Total: **46/60**

### ## Scorecard

| Category | Score | Read |
|---|---|---|
| Composition | 8/10 | Asymmetry feels earned; the horizon line carries the structure. |
| Color | 9/10 | Two-color piece. The restraint is the point. |
| Typography | 6/10 | Headline is right; the subtitle pairing is half-considered. |
| Hierarchy | 7/10 | Clear primary read, but the secondary read stalls. |
| Storytelling | 8/10 | "Anchored" is doing the work the photograph promises. |
| Emotional Impact | 8/10 | Sober, weighty, honest. Earns the feeling. |

### ## Why It Works / Why It Doesn't

The composition works because the horizon line is the grid. You've placed the title slightly above center, which lets the sea hold the bottom of the frame and the sky open the top — that's classical landscape composition repurposed for a series graphic. The eye lands on the title and then drops into the water. Good.

The color is the strongest move in the piece. A near-black sea against a slate sky is one tonal choice executed with discipline. You could have reached for blue and you didn't. That's restraint, and restraint is what gives the piece its gravity. Don't lose your nerve on this one.

Where it slips is the subtitle pairing. The thin condensed serif doesn't share a logic with the heavy sans-serif headline — it's a different temperature, a different century, and it sits at roughly 60% of the headline's height, which is too close in weight. The eye reads the headline, then is asked to choose between the subtitle and the wordmark, and chooses neither. The principle: when an element is meant to be secondary, it has to *visibly* defer.

### ## Specific Improvements

1. **Typography** — Drop the subtitle to roughly 35–40% of the headline's cap height and let it ride at one half the visual weight. Same typeface family if possible (the regular weight of your headline face), or a quiet humanist sans like Söhne Light. Right now the serif is competing for personality.
2. **Hierarchy** — Pull the subtitle to within 12–16px of the headline's baseline. The current gap reads like two separate ideas; tightening it makes the subtitle a clear second-tier element of the same idea.
3. **Composition** — Consider letting the photograph bleed slightly under the title (a 5–8% darken behind the type, not a flat scrim). Right now the title sits *on* the image; with a soft darken, it sits *in* it. The principle: type should feel weighted into its environment, not stickered onto it.
4. **Storytelling** — The wordmark in the bottom-left is fine, but it's at the same opacity as the subtitle. Drop it to ~60% white. The series should be louder than the church on this asset.

### ## Pattern Watch

First review of this session — no pattern yet.

### ## What I'd Borrow

The decision to keep this at two colors. The team should look at this and ask: *what would I lose if I removed the color?* For most pieces, the answer is "nothing." That's worth noticing.

## Example 2 — Announcement slide (Rework, 33/60)

*Imagined piece: A 1920×1080 slide for the Sunday lobby screens. Headline reads "VOLUNTEER SIGN-UPS THIS SUNDAY" in Bebas Neue, all caps, taking ~50% of the width. Below it, "Make a difference. Serve your city." in italicized Montserrat. Below that, a CTA "scan the QR code below" in a third typeface (a script). A QR code sits bottom-right. Background is a stock photograph of diverse smiling people in a circle, mid-saturation, with a semi-transparent blue overlay at ~30% opacity.*

### ## Creative Director Summary

This piece is trying to say four things and ends up saying none of them clearly. The hierarchy has collapsed — three competing typefaces, three competing tones, and a stock photo that flattens everything underneath. This is a rework, not a refine. The good news: the underlying message ("we need volunteers") is small and clear, so a stripped-down version of this slide would land much harder than the current one.

### ## Verdict

**Rework** — Total: **33/60**

### ## Scorecard

| Category | Score | Read |
|---|---|---|
| Composition | 5/10 | Centered stack. Functional, not built. |
| Color | 4/10 | Blue overlay is decorative; doesn't help the photo or the type. |
| Typography | 3/10 | Three faces with no relationship to each other. |
| Hierarchy | 4/10 | Headline reads first; everything after is a tie. |
| Storytelling | 6/10 | Idea is clear (we want volunteers); execution doesn't carry it. |
| Emotional Impact | 4/10 | Generic stock-photo warmth. Could be advertising anything. |

### ## Why It Works / Why It Doesn't

Storytelling is the strongest element here, which sounds backhanded but isn't — the message is plain and recoverable. The team knows what it wants this slide to say. That's the asset to build on.

The typography is where this piece breaks. Bebas Neue, italic Montserrat, and a script font occupy three different design centuries and three different attitudes. Bebas is a punchy headline face; Montserrat italic reads like ad-tech body copy; the script is decorative. None of them are wrong by themselves; together they read as default-mode design — the typefaces that show up when no one made a decision. The principle: every typeface in a piece should be there because it's doing a job no other typeface in the piece could do.

The blue overlay is doing real damage. The photo underneath is mid-saturation already, and a 30% blue scrim turns the whole piece into a single muddy hue without giving you the contrast you'd need for legibility. You're paying the cost of an overlay (flattened photo) without getting the benefit (clear type contrast).

### ## Specific Improvements

1. **Typography** — Cut to one typeface. A clean modern sans like Söhne, Inter, or Neue Haas Grotesk, used at three weights (Bold for the headline, Regular for the supporting line, Medium small caps for the CTA). One family does all three jobs and the piece will instantly feel built.
2. **Color** — Lose the blue overlay. Instead, pick one solid background color (a near-black, a deep brand color, or a desaturated warm gray) and place the photograph as a smaller, intentional element — say, 40% of the frame on the right, with type holding the left. The principle: a photo as background hides; a photo as an element communicates.
3. **Hierarchy** — Establish three tiers, not four. Headline → one line of supporting copy → CTA. The CTA gets a clear graphic cue (a button shape, or a horizontal rule above it) so the eye knows it's the action.
4. **Storytelling** — "Volunteer Sign-ups This Sunday" is the *ask*, not the *idea*. Try a headline that says what serving means, with the logistic (this Sunday, scan code) in the supporting copy. Example: *"Take your seat on the team."* Then in smaller type: *"Sign up this Sunday — scan to start."*
5. **Composition** — Anchor everything to a real grid. A simple two-column 16:9 layout (type left, image right, with a 5% margin) will fix half of what's broken before you change anything else.

### ## Pattern Watch

Second review this session. Both pieces this week have a typography problem — the first piece pairs a heavy sans with an unrelated serif; this piece pairs three unrelated faces. The team is treating typography as decoration. Worth a 30-minute team session on disciplined type pairing — one family, three weights, until it becomes muscle memory.

### ## What I'd Borrow

Nothing in the execution yet. But the *willingness to put a clear ask on a slide* (rather than burying the volunteer pitch in a paragraph) is the right instinct. The piece needs to catch up to the instinct.

## Example 3 — Instagram carousel cover (Restart, 24/60)

*Imagined piece: A 1080×1080 cover slide for an Instagram carousel titled "5 WAYS TO PRAY BETTER." The title is set in heavy serifed type with a chrome-gradient effect, centered. Behind it: a stock photo of hands folded over an open Bible, sun flare in the corner, heavily filtered with a warm orange-and-teal color grade. A small "swipe →" indicator in the bottom corner uses a different typeface again. The bottom 20% of the frame has a translucent white box with the church name in a thin sans-serif.*

### ## Creative Director Summary

This piece is decoration in search of a message. Every element is reaching for "spiritual" through cliché — chrome type, sun flare, orange-and-teal grade, hands-on-Bible stock photo — and the cumulative effect is that the piece looks like every other pray-more graphic on the platform. The recommendation is a clean restart: same headline idea, none of the current visual moves.

### ## Verdict

**Restart** — Total: **24/60**

### ## Scorecard

| Category | Score | Read |
|---|---|---|
| Composition | 4/10 | Centered stack with three weight zones competing. |
| Color | 3/10 | Orange-and-teal cliché is doing the talking. |
| Typography | 3/10 | Chrome-gradient serif. The effect *is* the type choice. |
| Hierarchy | 5/10 | Title reads first; nothing else needs to. |
| Storytelling | 4/10 | "Pray better" is generic; the visual makes it more so. |
| Emotional Impact | 5/10 | Reads as "spiritual content" — which is to say, indistinct. |

### ## Why It Works / Why It Doesn't

Hierarchy is the only category not in the cellar, and only because the title is so loud that everything else gives up gracefully. That's not a compliment — that's a single-element design.

The typography is where the piece announces itself as not yet ready for the feed. A chrome-gradient effect on a serif is using a built-in style preset to do the work that a *type choice* should do. The principle: an effect cannot replace a typeface decision. If you remove the chrome gradient, what's left is a generic serif at a medium weight — which means the type wasn't actually doing the job.

The color grade is the second injury. Orange-and-teal is the visual language of stock-photo Instagram. Combined with sun flare and folded hands, the piece is built entirely from genre conventions. There is no decision in this image that wasn't already made by the platform.

The most honest thing I can say: this isn't a piece that gets fixed. It's a piece that gets remade once the team decides what *praying better* actually means *to this church*. Without that conviction, the visual will keep reaching for cliché.

### ## Specific Improvements

1. **Storytelling first** — Before opening the design tool again, write one sentence: *"What does 'pray better' mean to us?"* If the answer is "more often," the piece becomes about repetition. If the answer is "more honestly," the piece becomes about quiet. The visual flows from that decision; nothing useful happens before it.
2. **Strip the visual moves** — Remove the chrome gradient, the sun flare, the orange-and-teal grade, and the stock photo of hands. None of them are decisions. Start with a blank 1080×1080 and one typeface.
3. **Composition** — Try the headline alone, set in a single quiet sans-serif at three weights, on a flat warm-cream or cool-near-black background. No image at all. The audacity of an empty Instagram cover that just *says the thing* is itself the design.
4. **Typography** — One face. Three weights max. Numerals in the same family. If the type can't carry the piece by itself, the headline isn't right yet — go back to step 1.

### ## Pattern Watch

Third review this session. Recurring issue: the team is reaching for *style* (overlays, filters, gradients, type effects) before *idea*. The pieces are decorated before they're decided. Suggested drill: for one week, every piece must be reviewed first as a wireframe — type only, no photos, no effects, no color beyond black/white/gray. The pieces that survive that first read will be stronger when the visual layer is added back.

### ## What I'd Borrow

Nothing yet — the foundation needs work first.

## Example 4 — Worship motion still (Ship It, 54/60)

*Imagined piece: A 16:9 still extracted from a slow-moving worship motion loop. A single word — "PEACE" — sits roughly center-right of the frame, set in a quiet humanist sans-serif at ~120pt, off-white. The rest of the frame is dark, near-black, with slow-drifting gray smoke and a soft, almost imperceptible warm glow at the lower right. Nothing else on screen. The word is slightly tracked open, breathing.*

### ## Creative Director Summary

This is the only way it could be. One word, one typeface, one color, one slow movement. The discipline is what makes it work; remove anything and you'd weaken it. Ship it as-is and let the team see what happens when restraint gets to do the talking.

### ## Verdict

**Ship It** — Total: **54/60**

### ## Scorecard

| Category | Score | Read |
|---|---|---|
| Composition | 9/10 | Off-center placement creates the breath. |
| Color | 9/10 | Two values: near-black and off-white. Nothing more is needed. |
| Typography | 9/10 | One face, opened slightly, doing all the work. |
| Hierarchy | 9/10 | One element. Hierarchy is moot — and that's the move. |
| Storytelling | 9/10 | The word and the silence around it are the same idea. |
| Emotional Impact | 9/10 | Stops the room. Earns the word. |

### ## Why It Works / Why It Doesn't

Composition is the first thing to admire. Placing "PEACE" off-center-right, with negative space dominating the left two-thirds, is the kind of asymmetry that has to be earned. The eye lands on the word and then is allowed to sit in the empty room around it. That empty room is not absence — it's the active ingredient.

The typography is doing exactly what it's supposed to and nothing else. A humanist sans at 120pt with light tracking is a quiet voice, which is what the word *peace* requires. A bolder face would have made the word announce itself; this version lets the word *be*.

The slow drift of the smoke and the imperceptible warm glow at the lower right are the only motion in the piece, and they're both subtractive — they recede instead of demanding attention. This is the discipline most teams skip: they add motion to fill time. This piece *uses* time.

If anything held it back from a 60/60, it's that the warm glow could be slightly stronger to give the eye one more thing to settle into on long views. But that's a 1-point conversation, not a fix.

### ## Specific Improvements

1. **Color** — Optional, not necessary: bring the warm glow at the lower right up by ~10–15% luminance for sets where the loop will play for more than 90 seconds. Gives the eye one more place to rest. For shorter loops, leave it as is.
2. **Typography** — On large rooms (>800 capacity), test a one-point increase in tracking. The added breath helps the word read across distance.
3. **Storytelling** — When this piece runs in a set, the word before and after it should be similarly disciplined. One word, same family. The set becomes the design.

### ## Pattern Watch

Fourth review this session. This is the strongest piece in the set, and the strongest principle in it — *one decision per piece* — is exactly what the earlier reviews were missing. Worth screening this for the whole team as the standard. The other three pieces this week each had four or five "design moves" working against each other; this one has one move and trusts it.

### ## What I'd Borrow

The willingness to hand the room over to a single word. Most worship visuals try to do too much because the team is afraid of being boring. This piece is the opposite of boring — it's the only piece in the set that the room would actually stop for. Borrow that nerve.
